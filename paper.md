# Summary

Storage Lab is a collection of reproducible benchmarks and the open datasets they
produce, covering the practical questions behind image storage: how much smaller
modern formats (WebP, AVIF, JPEG XL) are than JPEG at matched perceptual quality,
what converting a HEIC photo library to JPEG or PNG really costs in size, how
cloud storage price per gigabyte has moved, and how many photos a phone of a
given capacity holds. Each figure is generated by a single self-contained script
in `benchmarks/`, comparisons hold perceptual quality constant using the
structural similarity index (SSIM) [@wang2004ssim] against a lossless master, and
the resulting CSVs are published under CC BY 4.0 with the code under MIT.

# Statement of need

Claims about image-format savings are everywhere, but they are rarely
reproducible: vendor benchmarks use undisclosed corpora, and "X is 50% smaller"
figures usually omit the quality target that makes such a comparison meaningful.
Size and quality trade off continuously, so any comparison that does not fix
perceptual quality is uninterpretable. Practitioners deciding whether to adopt
AVIF or JPEG XL, and researchers studying the economics of media storage, lack an
openly licensed, auditable dataset that ties each size number to an explicit
quality criterion and a re-runnable method.

Storage Lab addresses this by publishing both the datasets and the exact scripts
that produce them, with SSIM-matched quality as a first-class part of the method.
Anyone can re-run a benchmark on their own corpus and get comparable numbers, or
cite the published CSVs directly.

# State of the field

General image-processing libraries (for example `sharp`, `libvips`, ImageMagick)
provide the encoders but no benchmark methodology or dataset. Codec projects
publish their own comparisons, but on private corpora and without a shared,
quality-controlled harness. Public discussions of "next-gen formats" typically
cite one-off blog measurements that cannot be reproduced or extended. To our
knowledge there is no openly licensed, script-reproducible dataset that fixes
perceptual quality via SSIM across JPEG, WebP, AVIF, and JPEG XL and also links
those savings to downstream storage-cost questions (the HEIC conversion tax,
cloud price per gigabyte, device capacity). Storage Lab fills that gap.

# Software design

Each benchmark is an independent Node.js script under `benchmarks/`, so a reader
can audit one measurement without understanding the whole repository:

- `compression-benchmark.mjs` and `nextgen-formats-benchmark.mjs` encode a corpus
  across formats and quality settings, computing SSIM against the lossless master
  so that reported size reductions are at matched perceptual quality.
- `heic-tax-benchmark.mjs` quantifies the size increase from converting a HEIC
  corpus to JPEG and PNG at matched quality.
- `cloud-price-index.mjs`, `photo-storage-capacity.mjs`, and
  `streaming-data-usage.mjs` compile supporting reference datasets.
- `render-charts.mjs` regenerates the figures in `docs/charts/` from the CSVs, so
  the charts are never hand-edited and always follow the data.

Every script writes a CSV into `data/`, which is the citable artifact. Encoding
uses `sharp` [@sharp]. Because inputs, method, and outputs are all explicit, a
result can be reproduced or challenged directly.

# Research impact statement

The datasets support decisions and studies that need quality-controlled,
openly licensed evidence rather than vendor claims: choosing an image format for
a product or archive, estimating the storage and bandwidth impact of a format
migration at scale, and modeling the economics of media storage over time. The
HEIC conversion tax dataset in particular quantifies a cost that millions of
users incur when exporting photos out of the Apple ecosystem, and is accompanied
by a preprint. Human-readable write-ups are published at
<https://cleanor.app/research> and the datasets are mirrored on Hugging Face and
Kaggle for reuse.
